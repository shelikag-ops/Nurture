## Values Framework: The Three Pillars

Three cross-cutting values woven through every subject and the daily dashboard. Not a separate subject — a lens applied to existing time. **Multicultural, not Sanskrit-only** — values feel discovered through what she's studying, not scheduled in advance.

| Pillar | Simple meaning | How she experiences it daily |
|---|---|---|
| **Right Conduct / Dharma** | Doing what's right, even when it's hard | Courage quests, debate, daily thought |
| **Selfless Service / Seva** | Giving without expecting in return | Kindness quests, history stories |
| **Gratitude / Contentment** | Appreciating what you have — specifically, not vaguely | Daily dashboard reflection with coaching |

### Daily Dashboard — Thought of the Day
~180 entries, non-sequential, multicultural. Sources: Hindu/Upanishadic wisdom, Confucius, Buddhist teachings, Stoics (Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus), Rumi/Sufi poets, Ubuntu, Native American perspectives, Gandhi, APJ Abdul Kalam, modern figures. Loosely connected to what she's studying in history when natural, freely chosen otherwise.

Format:
> **[Concept / quote]** — *[source / tradition]*
> "[One-sentence reflection prompt]"

Some entries are pure celebration: *"Name one thing you did this week that made you proud."*

### Gratitude Prompts — Coaching Layer (IMPORTANT)
Generic gratitude ("I'm grateful for my family, my house") misses the point. The goal is **specific, sensory, unexpected** gratitude. Claude coaches her when her response is too vague by asking one follow-up question:

**If she writes something generic, Claude responds with:**
- "That's a good start — can you name one specific moment this week when you felt that?"
- "What exactly did it feel like, or what did you see/hear/taste that made you grateful for that?"
- "What's something small and ordinary about that thing that you'd miss most?"

**Examples of generic vs. specific (shown to her as examples in the dashboard):**
- ❌ Generic: "I'm grateful for my family"
- ✅ Specific: "I'm grateful for when Mum laughed at my joke at dinner on Tuesday even though it wasn't that funny"
- ❌ Generic: "I'm grateful for my home"
- ✅ Specific: "I'm grateful for my bed and how it feels to get into it when I'm really tired"

The coaching is gentle, one question at a time, not a checklist. The goal is to build the *habit* of noticing specifics, not to pass a test.

### Courage Quest Expansion (Seva layer added)
Original quests: social confidence (talking to strangers, grocery store, returns counter) — keep all.
New Seva-tier quests (escalating badge series):
- 🌱 Seed: "Give a genuine compliment to someone who helped you this week"
- 🌿 Sprout: "Help someone carry something without being asked, before they ask"
- 🌳 Tree: "Write a short thank-you note to someone who helps our family regularly"
- 🌟 Banyan: "Organise or take part in a small act of service for someone outside the family"

### Celebration-First Design Principle
Applies to ALL tools, dashboards, and feedback:
- Dashboard opens with yesterday's wins, not today's pending
- Badges fire a celebration for every win, no matter how small
- Claude's corrections always lead with what she got right; coaching comes second
- Streak system shows "your best streak" on missed days — never "streak broken"
- Weekly parent view is a wins report first, gaps second
- Language in all tools: "You figured that out!", "Look how far you've come", "Great attempt — here's the next step"

## History / Social Studies

### Curriculum Spine
**Britannica Encyclopedia for Kids** — chronological world history
**Current position:** Early ancient civilizations — Mesopotamia / Egypt (as of March 2026)
**Format:** Parent-led, 3x/week, ~3 topics/pages per session + YouTube videos + weekly quiz with a friend

### Pacing
**Self-paced — no milestone targets.** The sequence and coverage below is a guide, not a schedule. As long as history sessions are happening consistently (most of the week), that is success. If a civilisation captures her imagination and they stay with it longer, that is the right call. There is no end-of-year requirement for history.

- 3 sessions/week × ~48 weeks = ~144 sessions at full pace (aspirational, not binding)
- Consistency is the only KPI

### Suggested Sequence (aspirational — not a schedule)

**Near-term: Ancient World**
- Finish Mesopotamia (Sumerians, Babylonians, Assyrians)
- Ancient Egypt (Pharaohs, pyramids, daily life, religion)
- *Values moment:* Egyptian concept of Ma'at — truth, justice, balance → connects to Dharma

**When ready: Ancient World (Part 2)**
- Ancient India — Indus Valley Civilization + Vedic Period
  - *Values deep-dive:* Chandragupta (Dharma), **Ashoka** (Seva after Kalinga — richest values lesson of the year). *"What would you have done in Ashoka's position?"*
- Ancient China — Confucius, the Silk Road
  - *Values moment:* Confucian Ren (benevolence) ≈ Seva; Golden Rule as universal value

**When ready: Classical World**
- Ancient Greece — democracy, philosophy, Olympics → Socrates and the Dharma of honest questioning
- Ancient Rome — republic, empire, fall → Marcus Aurelius and Stoic gratitude/duty

**When ready: Medieval & Early Modern World**
- Islamic Golden Age — Seva through hospitals, libraries, scientific knowledge-sharing
- Medieval India — Bhakti movement, Mirabai, Kabir → devotion as gratitude

**When ready: Modern World**
- Renaissance, age of exploration
- Gandhi — Satya, Ahimsa, Seva in one life. Most powerful single values lesson of the year.
- Contemporary world — human rights, environmental stewardship

### Weekly Quiz with a Friend
Keep exactly as-is. Once a month, frame as "history detectives" — she and her friend solve an unsolved mystery together (How were the pyramids built? What caused Rome to fall?). Adds critical thinking and collaboration.

### End-of-Civilisation Reflection
1. *"What did this civilisation get right that we should still be doing today?"*
2. *"What did they get wrong that we should never repeat?"*
3. *"Which person from this period would you most want to have dinner with, and what would you ask them?"*

1. **New words** — 40–60 words on vocabularystars.com (~20–30 min)
2. **Previous day's revision** — quick recall pass on yesterday's set (~10–15 min)
3. **vocab.com** — 30 min daily for reinforcement, context, and deeper encoding

**Total vocabulary time until NSF: ~1 hr/day, 6–7 days/week (~6–7 hrs/week)**

This is a temporary sprint. Once NSF exam is done, daily time drops significantly (maintenance pace, not sprint pace).

### Post-NSF: Competition Roadmap

After NSF exams, shift from sprint to a sustainable competition calendar. The goal is to participate in 2–3 vocabulary/spelling competitions per year and keep the skill sharpening between them.

#### Competitions to target

| Competition | Format | Who runs it | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NSF Vocabulary B / Spelling B** | Annual, 2,000-word list, multiple rounds | North-South Foundation | Next cycle: ~early 2027 |
| **Scripps National Spelling Bee** | School → Regional → National. Oral spelling under pressure. | Scripps / local school | School-level qualifying typically Oct–Feb each year |
| **Vocabulary Bowl** (vocabularystars.com) | Online, team-based vocabulary competition, ranked nationally | VocabularyStars | Check site for next tournament date |
| **Vocabulary.com Championships** | Online, individual, global ranking by speed and accuracy | Vocabulary.com | Ongoing + periodic championships |
| **Local / regional Spelling Bees** | Varies by school or homeschool group | Schools, libraries, homeschool orgs | Search locally once NSF season is over |

#### Post-NSF maintenance pace (May 2026 onwards)
- vocab.com: **15–20 min/day** (down from 30 — keep the habit, reduce the load)
- vocabularystars.com: **as needed per active competition word list** (no list = 2–3 days/week only)
- New competition word list acquisition: once enrolled in Scripps or next NSF cycle

**Total vocabulary time post-NSF: ~2–2.5 hrs/week (maintenance)**

### Word Study Approach (beyond memorisation)

Vocabulary is strongest when it's not just memorised but understood in context. Once the NSF sprint is done, the vocabulary practice should add:

- **Etymology layer** (roots and prefixes make 1,000 words easier to remember than 1,000 isolated words):
  - E.g. *spec-* = look → spectacle, inspect, prospect, suspect
  - One root per week, 5 words that share it
- **Sentence creation** — for 5 hardest words each week, she writes one sentence she'd actually use
- **vocab.com challenges** — her accuracy and streak statistics can be tracked for the weekly feedback report

## Daily & Weekly Feedback — Design Specification

This is a core feature of the daily dashboard and Notion database, not an afterthought. The plan is only useful if it adapts to what actually happens.

---

### How the Plan Updates Itself (Dynamic Plan Logic)

Every task completion she logs in the dashboard triggers an update to the live plan:

| Completion event | Plan response |
|---|---|
| Science chapter marked done | Chapter ticked off the roadmap; next chapter queued; pacing recalculated against March 2027 goal |
| Science assessment completed | Score saved; weak topics flagged for next session; assessment slot removed until next chapter complete |
| Math BA chapter finished | Next chapter queued; NSF alignment checked |
| Vocabulary words done today | Running total updated; estimated list-completion date recalculated |
| Mosdos unit finished | Next unit queued; Claude prompts the values-reflection question |
| Book finished | Reading log updated; next book suggested from list |
| Scratch project completed | Project ladder advanced; next project briefed |
| Chess games played | Stats pulled from Lichess/ChessKid API; coaching note queued for weekly report |
| History session done | Session count incremented; no milestone checking (self-paced) |

No completion = no update. The plan doesn't move forward on its own — it moves when she does.

---

### Daily Feedback (end of each study day)

Delivered via the daily dashboard at the end of each session. Short, celebration-first, ~2 minutes to read.

**Structure:**
1. 🏆 **Today's wins** — everything she completed, framed positively ("Finished 50 vocab words — 1,150 to go!")
2. 🔥 **Streaks** — any active streak she's maintaining (vocab days in a row, chess puzzles, reading)
3. 💬 **One thought** — a single observation or encouragement from Claude (personalised, never generic — references what she actually did)
4. 📅 **Tomorrow** — what's scheduled for tomorrow, in plain language

The daily feedback does NOT include: gaps, what she missed, how far behind she is. That goes in the weekly report, to the parent view only.

---

### Weekly Feedback (every Sunday, or Monday morning)

Two versions: **her view** (celebration + forward-looking) and **parent view** (full picture, including gaps and pacing risks).

**Her view (Sunday evening — 3–5 min read):**
- 🌟 **Week's biggest win** — one highlight singled out
- 📊 **Subject-by-subject emoji summary** — quick visual of the week (✅ on track, 🚀 ahead, ⏳ needs a push)
- 📚 **Reading update** — books finished, pages read, reading streak
- 🗓️ **Next week preview** — what's coming up, any special events flagged

**Parent view (weekly email / Notion page — more detailed):**
- Subject-by-subject pacing: actual vs. planned sessions this week
- **Science roadmap check**: chapters done to date vs. target — flag if >2 chapters behind
- **Vocabulary sprint tracker** (until NSF): words done, words remaining, estimated completion date
- **Any red flags** — if a subject has had zero completions for 5+ days, parent gets a quiet flag
- Suggested adjustment: if behind on science, Claude proposes where to find an extra session in next week's schedule
- Chess coaching summary: games played, rating trend, one specific skill to work on this week

---

### Pacing Alerts (automated, not nagging)

These are one-time flags, not recurring reminders. They appear once and wait for acknowledgement.

- 🟡 **Science pace warning**: Triggered if the roadmap shows <40 chapters completed by end of October 2026 (leaves insufficient time for remaining chapters)
- 🟡 **Vocabulary sprint warning**: Triggered if the list won't be done 2 weeks before the NSF exam date (once that date is known)
- 🟢 **Ahead of pace**: Triggered positively — "You're a chapter ahead of target — want to take it easy this week or keep the momentum?"
- 🔵 **Travel mode active**: No pacing alerts fire during travel mode

## Dashboard Feature Spec: Voice Scheduler, Homework Logging & Time Intelligence

These three features form a unified **time intelligence layer** on the daily dashboard. Together they answer the most important question in any homeschool plan: *is what's actually happening matching what we planned?*

---

### Voice Scheduler

A microphone button always visible on the dashboard. Powered by the Web Speech API — built into iPad Safari and Mac Chrome, no external service, no cost, works offline.

Sarah uses short spoken commands, nothing more:

| Command | What happens |
|---|---|
| *"Start maths"* | Timer begins; task marked in-progress on the dashboard |
| *"Done"* | Timer stops; task logged as complete; celebratory micro-animation plays |
| *"Move this to later"* | Current task pushed to next available slot; next task surfaces |
| *"Add homework: read chapter 4, due Thursday"* | Task created with deadline; auto-slotted into next available block; Shell flagged if no slot fits before deadline |
| *"What's next?"* | Dashboard reads back the next scheduled item |
| *"I need a break"* | Everything pauses; break timer starts; no guilt, just data |

Commands are forgiving — "starting vocab now", "start vocabulary", "vocab" all map to the same action. The parser is fuzzy, not literal.

**Why voice matters for her specifically:** She's independent and self-running, but she's 8. Typing a time log is friction she'll skip. Saying "done" takes one second and becomes a habit within a week.

---

### Homework Logging

Every class generates homework that currently lives nowhere between "teacher mentions it" and "she does it." The voice scheduler handles capture; the dashboard handles scheduling.

**Flow:**
1. After a class, she says *"Add homework: [description], due [day]"*
2. Dashboard creates the task, assigns it to the right subject bucket, and slots it into the next available block of that subject type before the deadline
3. If the week is too full to fit it, Shell gets a quiet flag: *"Science assessment + Mosdos homework both due Thursday — one needs to move"*
4. Parent can override the auto-slot at any time

Homework tasks are visually distinct from planned curriculum tasks in the dashboard — different colour, marked "📌 Class homework."

---

### Activity Time Tracker

Every Start/Done pair produces a time log entry automatically. No manual input required beyond the two voice commands.

**What gets logged:** subject, start time, end time, duration, source (planned / homework / free choice)

**Where it goes:** Notion database (primary), localStorage (fallback if Notion isn't connected)

**How it's used:**
- Daily view: total active study time today, breakdown by subject
- Weekly parent report: planned time vs. actual time, side by side, per subject
- Monthly plan review: persistent gaps between planned and actual trigger a curriculum adjustment recommendation

*Example:* Plan says 1 hour of Jacob's Ladder per week. Time tracker shows she's averaging 22 minutes. That's not a discipline problem — it's a signal that the sessions are either too short (she finishes early), too hard (she quits), or misscheduled (she's tired at that time). The data points to which one.

---

### Screen Time Integration

Apple does not expose Screen Time data through a public API. The practical approach that actually works:

**Weekly process (2 minutes):**
1. On Sunday, Shell or Sarah screenshots the weekly Screen Time summary from iPad Settings and from Mac System Settings → Screen Time
2. Upload both screenshots here
3. Claude reads the app-by-app and category breakdown and maps it against the activity log for the same week

**Output — "Planned vs. Actual" overlay:**

| Subject | Planned | Activity log | Screen Time shows |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maths | 10 hrs | 9.5 hrs | Khan Academy: 8 hrs, Notability: 1.5 hrs ✅ |
| Vocabulary | 7 hrs (sprint) | 6.8 hrs | VocabularyStars: 3.2 hrs, Vocab.com: 3.6 hrs ✅ |
| Scratch | 3 hrs | 2.2 hrs | Scratch website: 1.1 hrs ⚠️ |
| YouTube | — | — | YouTube: 2.4 hrs 🔍 *What was she watching?* |

The yellow and red flags go to the parent view only. Sarah's view just shows her wins.

**Monthly plan review:** Screen time data is more useful as a monthly pattern than a weekly snapshot. After 4 weeks of data, Claude can identify: which hours of the day she's most focused (consistent start times), which subjects she naturally extends beyond the plan, and what's consistently under-logged (probably means she finds it unpleasant). These feed into the monthly plan adjustment.
